# Species, Weight, Age, and Medical History Notes

## Why this comes up

Owners often ask whether a medication used for one pet can be used for another pet in the house. From a pharmacy perspective, that is a warning sign, not a shortcut.

For pets, the label should be read as pet-specific, not household-generic. The pet name on the bottle matters. So do species, current weight, age, diagnosis, medical history, other medications, and the veterinarian’s most recent instruction.

## Information to have ready

Before calling the veterinary office or pharmacy, write down:

- Pet name
- Species
- Current weight
- Age or life stage
- Medication name and form
- Why the veterinarian prescribed it
- Other medications or supplements
- Other pets in the house
- Recent vomiting, diarrhea, appetite change, tiredness, swelling, breathing issues, or behavior change

The pharmacy can help read the label. The veterinarian interprets what those details mean for the pet.

## Questions for the veterinarian

Ask the veterinary office:

- Does this medication still fit my pet’s current weight and condition?
- Do other pets in the house need their own evaluation?
- Does age, kidney history, liver history, diabetes, pregnancy, seizure history, allergy history, or breed sensitivity change the plan?
- What signs should make me call before the next refill?
- Is a recheck or lab test needed?

## Questions for the pharmacy

Ask the pharmacy:

- Does the bottle label name the correct pet?
- Does the refill number show more medication available?
- Does the form on the label match what I have at home?
- Are there storage notes, shake-well notes, refrigeration notes, or beyond-use date details?
- Can the pharmacy send a clear refill request to the veterinary office?

## Common points of confusion

Two pets may share a home, a species, or a medication name, but they do not share a chart. A large dog and a small dog are not interchangeable. A cat and a dog are not interchangeable. A current pet and a previous pet are not interchangeable.

Do not split household medications between pets. Do not use an old bottle because the medication name sounds familiar.

## When not to wait

If a pet received another pet’s medication, contact the veterinary office or emergency veterinary care. Have the bottle label ready and report the time, amount if known, and pet details.

## A practical pharmacy note

The pharmacy may see the medication record, but the veterinary office sees the pet’s clinical context. That difference matters. A bottle label can tell us what was dispensed; it cannot tell us whether a new symptom, changed weight, or new diagnosis changes the plan.

For that reason, species and weight questions are not paperwork. They are part of keeping the medication tied to the right animal.

Current weight is not a courtesy detail. It may affect how the veterinary office thinks about the prescription, the product size, the monitoring plan, or whether the pet needs to be seen again. The pharmacy should not guess from a previous weight when the owner says the pet has grown, lost weight, or recently became ill.
