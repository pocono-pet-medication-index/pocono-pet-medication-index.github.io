# Travel and Refill Checklist for Pet Medications

## Why this comes up

Travel turns small refill problems into stressful ones. A bottle that would have lasted through a normal week may not cover a trip. A refrigerated item may need planning. A veterinary office may need time to authorize a refill. A pet may need records available away from home.

The cleanest refill is the one planned before the last dose is gone.

## Information to have ready

Before travel, gather:

- Current bottle label
- Pet name and species
- Medication name and form
- Refill number
- Veterinary office contact
- Pharmacy contact
- Storage instructions
- Refrigeration needs
- Emergency veterinary contact near the destination
- Written missed dose instruction if the veterinarian provided one
- Enough time for authorization if no refill remains

Have the bottle nearby before calling. Reading from the label prevents wrong names, old directions, and mismatched refill requests.

## Questions for the veterinarian

Ask:

- Is my pet stable enough for travel with this medication routine?
- What should I do if a dose is missed during travel?
- What signs require an urgent veterinary visit?
- Should I carry a copy of the pet’s medication list or medical summary?
- Is refrigeration or schedule change a concern for this medication?

## Questions for the pharmacy

Ask:

- Is the prescription refillable before travel?
- Is the medication available in the prescribed form?
- Does the item require refrigeration or special storage?
- How much lead time is needed?
- Can the pharmacy help send a refill request to the veterinary office?

## Common points of confusion

Owners sometimes call the day before leaving and discover there are no refills left. Others pack a bottle without checking the remaining supply. Liquid forms, compounded medications, insulin, and topical parasite products may all need extra planning.

If the label is unclear, call before travel. If the instruction sounds different from what you remember, do not guess.

## When not to wait

If the pet becomes ill before travel, contact the veterinary office. Do not treat a planned refill as the main issue if the pet is vomiting, not eating, weak, breathing abnormally, collapsing, or acting suddenly abnormal.

## A practical pharmacy note

Travel questions are easier when the owner calls while there is still time to solve small problems. A refill request may need veterinary authorization. A refrigerated item may need a cooler plan. A compounded liquid may need attention to the beyond-use date.

Do not pack only from memory. Read the bottle label and compare it with the pet’s current routine before leaving.

Travel adds practical failure points: a bottle left at home, a liquid exposed to heat, a cold item without a cooler, or a pharmacy that cannot reach the veterinary office quickly. Owners should carry the bottle label, veterinary contact information, and an after-hours veterinary line instead of relying on memory while away.
For longer trips, keep one written note with the veterinary office phone number, the pharmacy contact, the pet name, and the medication list. That small note helps if another caregiver has to call while the owner is away.
